question:What is the average (mean) bpp per image frame achieved using SOTA video compression techniques?

answer:As of my last update in 2023, assessing the average bits per pixel (bpp) per image frame achieved using state-of-the-art (SOTA) video compression techniques involves several variables, including the complexity of the video content, the resolution of the video, the frame rate, and the specific goals of the compression (e.g., maintaining visual quality versus minimizing storage space). Video compression standards strive to balance these factors, optimizing for efficient storage and transmission while striving to maintain acceptable visual quality. # Video Compression Standards: 1. **H.264/AVC (Advanced Video Coding)**: This has been one of the most widely adopted video compression standards, used across a broad range of applications from online video streaming to Blu-ray discs. The bits per pixel for content compressed under H.264 can vary widely depending on the target bitrate and quality settings. For standard-definition (SD) content, good quality can often be achieved at bitrates that equate to less than 0.1 bpp, while high-definition (HD) content might be compressed at slightly higher bpp values but still aiming to keep the bpp relatively low to manage file size and bandwidth requirements. 2. **H.265/HEVC (High Efficiency Video Coding)**: HEVC is the successor to H.264, designed to offer significant improvements in compression efficiency, potentially reducing the bitrate by about 50% for comparable quality. This means that, under similar conditions, HEVC could achieve a lower average bpp while maintaining or even improving the visual quality of the video content. 3. **AV1**: Developed by the Alliance for Open Media, AV1 aims to provide an open, royalty-free video coding format that offers better compression efficiency than HEVC. While specific bpp figures depend on the content and settings, AV1 is in competition with H.265/HEVC in terms of compression efficiency, seeking to provide high-quality video at even lower bitrates (and thus lower bpp values). 4. **VVC (Versatile Video Coding)**: As the newest standard, VVC, also known as H.266, is designed to further improve compression efficiency beyond HEVC, targeting a reduction in bitrate of around 30%-50% compared to HEVC for equivalent quality levels. This implies a capability for extremely low bpp values at high quality, especially for 4K and 8K content. # Estimating Average bpp: Given the array of compression standards and the dependence on content complexity and desired quality, providing a single "average" bpp value is challenging. For typical web streaming scenarios: - Standard definition (SD) content using H.264/AVC might be around 0.05 to 0.2 bpp. - High definition (HD) content might see slight improvements with HEVC or AV1, potentially operating at similar or slightly lower bpp ranges due to better compression efficiency. - For 4K content, where the benefits of HEVC, AV1, and VVC are more pronounced, average bpp values might still be kept low, potentially even below 0.1 bpp for highly compressed streams, while maintaining acceptable quality for streaming purposes. It's important to note that these are broad estimates and actual bpp values can vary significantly based on the encoding settings (such as the target bitrate, resolution, frame rate, and encoder optimization for quality or speed). Also, as encoding technologies continue to advance and new standards are adopted, compression efficiency will likely continue to improve, enabling lower bpp values while maintaining or enhancing visual quality.
